Fun things to do with the computer model

This page will give you fun ideas for using the computer model. Remember, all of these shapes are what you would see by standing on the virtual space station. From an outside perspective, the object in motion actually travels in a straight line across the spinning environment. Let’s have some fun by imagining ourselves on this space station.

Try it!

Diameter=50 ft

Start height=4 ft

Angle=0°

Speed=0 ft/s

Gravity=100%

  • Try dropping the coin instead of throwing it (throw it at 0 ft/s). It curves in the opposite direction.

  • Next, try dropping the coin from almost the center of the station. With the diameter at 50 feet, place the starting height at 24.5 feet. Imagine you are standing on a tall pole and drop the coin while rotating near the center. It appears to spiral outward slowly due to the low gravity near the center. The coin actually travels in a straight path, but the floor–where we are observing from–rotates relative to the coin.

A gargantuan spinning space station

Let’s see what happens when we increase the size of our virtual space station to 1,000 km. In theory, increasing the size of the space station will decrease the error due to the Coriolis effect. This section will show you that even the most massive space stations have visible error due to the Coriolis effect.

Try it!

Diameter=1000000 m

Start height=1.2 m

Angle=0°

Speed=4 m/s

Gravity=100%

First, let’s try our original toss. We find it now has only about 3 millimeters of distortion!

Objects that have a high speed in relation to the spinning speed continue to be distorted in their path.

How large does the station need to be for the model to read 0 millimeters of error with a coin toss? At 50,000,000 meters diameter (50,000 km), the model reads 0.04 cm of error, which rounds to 0 millimeters.

In summary, a massive space station could dramatically decrease the amount of error due to the Coriolis effect; however, high-speed objects would continue to be distorted depending on their launch angle and velocity. A coin toss on such a space station would appear as it would on Earth.
