The math behind this model: “difference from throw on Earth”

My purpose in creating a computer model was to learn more about a rotating environment. As I began building the model, I wanted a simple go-to measurement for the difference between a rotating throw and a throw on Earth. This provided me a fascinating physics problem. I wanted to know, if a person were to toss a coin straight upward in a rotating environment, how much would the Coriolis effect distort the toss? How far away would the coin land from their feet?

Set up the problem

To fully understand what happens during our coin toss, we must use a perspective that is not rotating. When beginning to solve a physics problem, it is important to establish a coordinate system. This helps to make sense of what is going on.

The station is a virtual coordinate system with (0,0) at its exact center and the thrower at the very bottom. I imagined the station rotating clockwise.

Here are some conditions for this coin toss:

  • At the moment of the throw, the person is standing at the bottom of the circle, at 270 degrees or 3π/2 radians.
  • The person is holding the coin at 4 feet from the floor. This is the starting height.
  • The person throws the coin straight upward at 14 ft/s, which would have sent the coin about 3 feet up in the air on Earth.
  • The rotating space station has a diameter of 100 feet. It is rotating at the speed necessary to produce 1g of acceleration, or 100% Earth gravity at the floor.
  • The coin experiences less than this amount of centripetal acceleration or “gravity” because it is not at the floor.

The next picture is a diagram of the velocity components for the throw. Before the throw, the coin has a leftwards velocity ($V_x$) due to the clockwise rotation; the object also experiences the centripetal force upwards ($F_c$). The throw adds an upwards velocity of 14 ft/s (the throw speed); the centripetal force ceases. The velocity components define the overall direction that the coin travels through the air ($V$), which we will need to calculate so we can determine where it lands.

Steps to solve the problem

Now that I have set up the problem, I will explain the next steps. Ideally, I would like a single equation that can solve the problem for me. Unfortunately, such an equation would be messy and impossible to simplify, so we will solve this in steps. We want to find how far away a coin would land from the thrower’s feet. To find this, we need to find the coordinates of where both the thrower and the coin would end up.

First, we need the magnitude and direction of the coin’s velocity after it launches. Using this, we can solve for its landing coordinate and the time it elapsed in the air. We can then use the time elapsed in the air to find where the thrower would end up in an equivalent time period. We can use this to find the separation between the coin and the thrower’s feet.

Here are the steps to solving our math problem:

  1. Calculate the magnitude and direction of the coin’s velocity through the air
  2. Find the coin’s landing coordinates
  3. Calculate the time elapsed as the coin traveled through the air
  4. Calculate the arc swept by the thrower during this same time
  5. Calculate the thrower’s final coordinates
  6. Calculate the distance between the thrower’s feet and the coin where it lands


Starting velocity

We need the vertical and horizontal velocity components of the coin. The vertical velocity has been given to us as 14 ft/s, the throw speed. The horizontal velocity is currently unknown, but it is based on the speed it was rotating before the throw. Using this information, we can calculate which direction the coin is moving.

Before we solve for the coin’s initial horizontal velocity, please note that the coin does not feel the same force of “gravity” as the person’s feet do on the floor of the station. Its distance to the station’s center is smaller than the station’s radius because you are holding it in your hand. The coin starts at a height of 4 ft from the floor. Thus, the coin’s horizontal velocity would not be quite the same as if it were touching the floor.

The coin’s landing coordinates

We can use the velocity components of the coin to find its landing coordinates. The coin is traveling up and to the left in a straight line; the slope of the path it follows is the fraction of the two velocity components, $\frac{v_y}{v_x}$. The landing coordinates will be where this line intersects with the station, a circle.

The equation for our line in slope-intercept form: $y = mx - r_c$

Our variables:

  • $m$: the slope, or $\frac{v_y}{v_x}$. It will be negative because the coin is moving left and up.
  • $r_c$: the y-intercept. This is the radius where the coin starts, mapped to our coordinate system. This is subtracted because it is below the center.

The person’s final coordinates

We can use the time variable to calculate the angle a person will rotate while the coin is in the air. This will give us the final coordinates of the person.

We have already calculated the station’s rotation rate in the beginning of our problem. This is $\omega$, in radians/second, and this is negative because the station is moving clockwise (counterclockwise is positive). Remember, $\omega=-t*\sqrt{\frac{a}{r}}$.

If $\theta_p$ is the total arc swept by the person, then you would expect $\theta_p=\theta_f-\theta_0$, the final minus the initial angles. The person starts at an angle of $\frac{3\pi}{2}$ (270 degrees). The person is also sweeping a negative arc.

This tells us that the coin landed 2.393 feet away from the person’s feet.

This value is the straight-line distance to the coin from the person’s feet. The model shows a scale along the floor; if you follow the tic-marks on the screen, you will discover the curvilinear distance to the coin, which may be slightly different. The model also calculates the curvilinear distance; you can find this in the stats menu.

The model breaks up the path of the coin into about 100 pieces, reorients each of these points to the person’s perspective at that moment, and moves real-time between these points to make an arc on the screen. The next article discusses how this is done.

Conclusion

It takes complex calculations just to understand a simple motion in a spinning environment; thus, it seems to me that Coriolis effects and forces are best understood with computer models.
